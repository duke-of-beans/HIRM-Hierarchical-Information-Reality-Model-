### 2.2 Phase Transition Theory and Critical Phenomena

Phase transitions describe qualitative state changes in systems, characterized mathematically through order parameters (macroscopic quantities distinguishing phases), control parameters (external variables driving transitions), and critical exponents (power-law scalings near critical points). **Second-order phase transitions** exhibit continuous order parameter changes with diverging correlation lengths and critical slowing down, while **first-order transitions** show discontinuous jumps with coexisting phases and hysteresis.

Brain dynamics exhibit both transition types: **gradual loss of consciousness under anesthesia** suggests second-order transitions with critical slowing and increased susceptibility, while **conscious access** exhibits all-or-none characteristics suggesting first-order or bifurcation transitions (Sergent et al., 2021; Klatzmann et al., 2025). Understanding which transition type governs consciousness emergence remains central to mechanistic theories.

Renormalization group (RG) theory provides powerful tools for analyzing critical systems across scales. Werner (2012) proposed consciousness emerges through sequential phase transitions along RG trajectories toward fixed points, where fully conscious states correspond to critical fixed points with maximal correlation density spanning fractal scales. Order parameters vary continuously with control parameters (e.g., anesthetic concentration), exhibiting meta-stability and hysteresis during transitionsâ€”experimentally observed phenomena that support phase transition frameworks.

### 2.3 Bifurcation Dynamics and Conscious Transitions

The breakthrough insight from 2025 research is that **phase transitions in consciousness are not gradual but bifurcations**â€”qualitative state changes where neural activity switches between discrete attractors (Klatzmann et al., 2025; Sergent et al., 2021). Prefrontal and parietal associative cortex maintains elevated NMDA-to-AMPA ratios (validated by autoradiography), creating bistability between low and high activity states.

**Conscious access occurs when inputs tip the system across bifurcation thresholds**â€”explaining the all-or-none character of conscious perception. ## 3. Empirical Evidence Across Scales and Species

### 3.1 Neuronal Avalanches: The Experimental Bedrock

**Neuronal avalanchesâ€”cascades of activity following power-law distributionsâ€”constitute the experimental foundation of brain criticality** (Beggs & Plenz, 2003). Beggs & Plenz (2004) extended these findings, showing neuronal avalanches are diverse and precise activity patterns stable for many hours in cortical slice cultures. The cascades exhibit remarkable temporal precision, with millisecond-scale coordination across millimeter-scale distances. This spatiotemporal precision suggests functional significance beyond mere statistical phenomena.

**Recent work reveals avalanches contain recurrent structure**. Salners et al. (2023) demonstrated large avalanches contain substantial recurrent activity and cyclic patterns absent in smaller events. Using transfer entropy to extract causal webs, they showed a dynamical weakening mechanismâ€”temporary threshold reductionâ€”replicates experimental distributions and moves the avalanche size exponent from 1/2 to 1, matching the de-pinning universality class. Arviv et al. (2019) revealed neuronal avalanches in stimulus-evoked activity across multiple time-frequency representations. Avalanches appear in both spontaneous and evoked conditions, suggesting critical dynamics persist during active information processing rather than only in "resting" states. The coexistence of stimulus-driven responses and avalanche-like propagation indicates criticality provides a dynamic substrate for computation.

### 3.2 Critical Dynamics During Behavior

A central question: do critical dynamics persist during behavior, or does task performance drive systems away from criticality? Yu et al. (2017) resolved this apparent paradox using adaptive binning where temporal resolution matches activity rate. Scale-invariance maintains during behavior when properly measured. Fixed binning spuriously suggests supercriticality during high-activity epochs, but phase synchronization levels remain constantâ€”**rate changes, not synchrony changes, drive apparent deviations**.

This finding has profound implications: brains maintain proximity to criticality even during demanding cognitive tasks. The apparent loss of critical signatures during behavior in earlier studies reflected methodological artifacts rather than genuine departures from criticality. Critical dynamics provide the computational substrate for flexible, adaptive behavior rather than only characterizing "idling" brain states.

### 3.3 The 2024 Breakthrough: Low-Dimensional Criticality in High-Dimensional Dynamics

Fontenele et al. (2024) resolved a fundamental paradox in a landmark *Science Advances* paper: how can brains simultaneously exhibit desynchronized, high-dimensional dynamics (required for rich information processing) and low-dimensional critical dynamics (required for avalanche signatures)?

**The resolution: critical and asynchronous dynamics coexist in orthogonal subspaces.** A low-dimensional subspace exhibits large fluctuations consistent with criticality at long timescales, while high-dimensional desynchronized activity optimizes input discrimination in orthogonal dimensions. Using recordings from awake behaving mice, they demonstrated that large avalanches (traditionally interpreted as global synchronization) actually reflect fluctuations in a low-dimensional subspace while preserving asynchronous, information-rich dynamics in the high-dimensional complement.

This finding reconciles seemingly contradictory observations: brains can be both critically poised (for optimal dynamic range and sensitivity) and informationally rich (for complex computations). The geometric structure of neural state space enables these apparently incompatible properties to coexist.

## 4. Self-Organization Mechanisms: How Brains Achieve Criticality

### 4.1 The Central Mechanistic Puzzle

**How do neural networks self-tune to criticality without external parameter adjustment?** This represents the field's most profound gap (Priesemann et al., 2014). Classical SOC systems (sandpiles, earthquakes) rely on external driving forces. Brains must generate critical dynamics through internal mechanisms, requiring local processes that somehow infer global system stateâ€”an apparent impossibility given information accessible to single neurons or synapses is necessarily local.

Three classes of mechanisms have been proposed, operating at different timescales: (1) short-term synaptic plasticity (hundreds of milliseconds), (2) long-term homeostatic plasticity (hours to days), and (3) structural plasticity and network reconfiguration (days to weeks). These mechanisms likely operate synergistically across timescales to maintain criticality under varying conditions.

### 4.2 Short-Term Synaptic Depression and Resource Depletion

Levina et al. (2007) demonstrated in *Nature Physics* that **synaptic depressionâ€”depletion of readily-releasable vesicle poolsâ€”creates negative feedback preventing runaway excitation**. Large avalanches deplete synaptic resources, suppressing subsequent events. This mechanism operates on hundreds-of-milliseconds timescales and broadens the critical parameter range.

However, short-term plasticity produces "hovering" around criticality rather than stable critical states. When plasticity is disabled, critical statistics disappear, indicating the mechanism does not establish permanent criticality but rather dynamically maintains it through continuous regulation. The system requires ongoing activity to sustain homeostatic balanceâ€”criticality as dynamic equilibrium rather than static configuration.

### 4.3 Long-Term Homeostatic Plasticity

Zeraati et al. (2021) distinguished plasticity mechanisms by persistence: **long-term homeostatic regulation creates stable critical states persisting after plasticity mechanisms are turned off**. Ma et al. (2019) provided landmark in vivo experimental validation in a *Neuron* paper. Critically, **inhibitory plasticity and PV+ interneuron regulation proved essential**, with excitatory dynamics recovering before firing rate homeostasis. This demonstrates active homeostatic tuning to a criticality setpoint in living animals.

The timescales match theoretical predictions: hours to days for homeostatic mechanisms to restore criticality after perturbations. However, dopamine likely represents one of multiple neuromodulatory systems regulating criticality. Acetylcholine, norepinephrine, and serotonin all show promise as criticality modulators. Costa et al. (2025) revealed that **hierarchical modular topology creates self-referential control at multiple scales**. ## 5. The Consciousness-Criticality Connection

### 5.1 From Correlation to Causation

Early evidence establishing the consciousness-criticality relationship was primarily correlational: conscious states exhibit critical dynamics, unconscious states do not. The 2024-2025 period saw a shift toward **causal evidence** through three converging lines: (1) pharmacological manipulation of criticality alters consciousness (Toker et al., 2022), (2) anesthetic-specific dissociations reveal necessity (Maschke et al., 2024), and (3) clinical interventions targeting criticality can restore consciousness (Dagnino et al., 2024).
